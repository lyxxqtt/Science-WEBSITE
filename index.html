Introduction to Plants

There are two major groups of plants: vascular and nonvascular plants. Nonvascular plants, such as mosses and liverworts, were among the first plants but never developed strong land adaptations, lacking root systems and vascular tissues. As a result, they are small, limited by short-distance water conduction. In contrast, vascular plants, including ferns and seed-producing plants, have specialized systems. Seed plants are classified into gymnosperms (naked seeds) and angiosperms (true seeds), with angiosperms further divided into monocots (one seed leaf) and dicots (two seed leaves).

The Difference Between Plants and Animals

Plants differ significantly from animals, often leading to misconceptions about their vitality. Unlike animals, plants do not gather food, move to escape danger, or physically confront predators. Instead, they remain rooted, relying on passive defense and growth strategies to survive.

Core Needs of a Plant to Survive

  • Sunlight – Provides energy for photosynthesis.
  • Water and Minerals – Water is a raw material for photosynthesis, while minerals from the soil support growth and development.
  • Gas Exchange – Plants need oxygen for respiration and carbon dioxide for photosynthesis.
  • Movement of Water and Nutrients – Specialized tissues transport water and nutrients from roots and distribute photosynthesis products throughout the plant.
